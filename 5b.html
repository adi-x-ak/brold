Best mysteries and thrillers of 2021

Al Woodworth|November 15, 2021


In her new book, Rebel Homemaker, Barrymore shares recipes, stories from her life, and personal photos to show how she leads a joyful and healthy life—and a rebellious one, too. To celebrate her new lifestyle book, we asked Barrymore about the book’s she’s read and loved recently. This is what she said:

Happy reading!

1) The Plot

by Jean Hanff Korelitz


Amazon Editor Seira Wilson raved about The Plot from the start, describing it as a “riveting novel […], a story within a story that is a Rubik’s Cube of twists.” It is, she wrote, “a psychological thriller [that] keeps the reader guessing even when the answers seem clear, and when the final piece clicks into place, what you see is the last thing you’d expect. The tale of a professor who steals the plot of a novel written by one of his deceased students is intriguing. And Korelitz makes the most of it, deftly sprinkling the plot with head spinning reveals and twists.

2) Razorblade Tears

by S.A. Cosby


S.A. Cosby’s Blacktop Wasteland, was our top mystery pick of 2020. With Razorblade Tears, Cosby once again delivers a searing novel full of heart and hurt which is also a propulsive, unputdownable thriller. Fueled by as much by self-recrimination as anger, two old ex-cons set out to avenge the murders of their gay sons. With prose as relentless as the woodchipper that makes an appearance early on, Cosby has us hooked yet again.

View details >
3) We Begin at the End

by Chris Whitaker


In the case of We Begin at the End, several of us read it simultaneously and were so wowed that soon the entire team was reading it. And we all agreed it was terrific. The plot in broad strokes is simple: tragic single mom Star Radley is murdered. But Whitaker builds a rich tapestry of supporting characters to play the story out. And chief among them is Star’s young daughter, Duchess—Whitaker gives us a riveting portrait in defiant vulnerability and fierce love—who readers will remember long after the last page is turned.

4) The First Day of Spring

by Nancy Tucker


Don’t be fooled by the benign sounding title of The First Day of Spring. This novel moves from horror to heartbreak to humor, often in the same page. We spend the novel inside the mind of a little girl named Chrissie, as a child and later, as a confused adult. Abandoned for long spells by her father, ignored, abused, and left to go hungry by her bitter, narcissistic mother, Chrissie is a gaping void of need. And that need propels her to murder.

5) The Man Who Died Twice

by Richard Osman


Pitch-perfect characters, witty, sharp, smart plotlines, and rib-achingly funny dialog, made selecting The Thursday Murder Club as a Best Mystery and Thriller of the month pick last year a no-brainer. Steven Spielberg agrees: he’s adapting it for the big screen. And the second in series, The Man Who Died Twice, picks up the baton beautifully. Our four septuagenarian sleuths are still friends, still getting older with gusto, and still living at Cooper’s Chase, the fancy retirement home where they gather as The Thursday Murder Club. Only this time, a case from Elizabeth’s past pops up—along with her ferrety ex-husband—and The Thursday Murder Club swings—carefully, mind you—into action again.
