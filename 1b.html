The Best nonfiction of November

Chris Schluep|November 9, 2021


November is traditionally a great month for publishing. There are so many big books released during this time, we editors tend to get a little excited about the prospects for great reading. This month’s list of the Best Nonfiction of the Month proves our excitement was not in vain. See some choice picks below, and be sure to check out the full list here.


Happy reading!

1) The Dawn of Everything: A New History of Humanity

by David Graeber and David Wengrow


This book is all the rage right now. Graeber, an anarchist-leaning anthropologist who helped to found the Occupy Wall Street movement (and who passed away in 2020) and archeologist Wengrow argue that the Big History view of gradual human improvement over time isn’t necessarily true. Things have been up and down for literal millennia, and what we have now isn’t necessarily what was bound to be. This is interesting, thought provoking food for thought..

2) Entertaining Race: Performing Blackness in America

by Michael Eric Dyson


Michael Eric Dyson looks at the history of being Black in America, beginning with the story of a young Black girl who was whipped to death on a slave ship because she would not entertain her white captors. This image raises the question of performance and judgement, and how the two have been used to define the Black experience on so many different levels. This is next level writing and thinking, full of moments and realizations that you will carry with you, and that will change your own thinking.

3) Tinderbox: HBO's Ruthless Pursuit of New Frontiers

by James Andrew Miller


James Andrew Miller has made a career out of access. His oral histories of ESPN and Saturday Night Live were both huge hits that drip dripped the words and revelations of the staff members, known and unknown, who made those shows famous. You can’t finish Those Guys Have All the Fun, Live from New York, or his Hollywood agenting book Power House without feeling you know those settings well and deeply, and that you could speak endlessly about them at a cocktail party. Now the author turns his attention to HBO, home of Game of Thrones, The Sopranos, Succession, and so much more. Buckle up.

4) Speaking of Race: Why Everybody Needs to Talk About Racism—and How to Do It

by Celeste Headlee

Who else to lead us in a discussion of race but a light-skinned Black Jew? That’s how PBS host Celeste Headlee describes herself, and it’s something that has caused her to confront questions of race and identity since she was a child. Surprisingly, Headlee notes that people don’t really want to engage in conversations about race. What they want is to talk about race with people who agree with them. But that will get us nowhere—so cue the subtitle of this book: Why Everybody Needs to Talk About Racism—and How to Do It. Headlee’s goal is to give us the tools to speak across the table with those who don’t really agree with us. This is an honest, straight forward, and reasonable book. It’s what it intends to be, which is evidence-based and helpful. Speaking of Race even includes examples of conversations with each chapter (from interviews she has conducted). They alone are worth your attention.

5) The Least of Us: True Tales of America and Hope in the Time of Fentanyl and Meth

by Sam Quinones

I remember our own Seira Wilson, six or seven years ago, talking (slightly nonstop) about a new book called Dreamland, which by her account was a really great read. The subtitle was The True Tale of America's Opiate Epidemic, and that alone probably stopped a few on the team from reading it back when it was published—but history has always judged Seira’s taste well, and now the book is an award winner, a big seller, and the basis for a hit Hulu series. The author of Dreamland is Sam Quinones, and he is now following up Dreamland with The Least of Us, which examines the synthetic drugs sweeping our country and suggests we might find a brighter way forward by focusing on reinforcing and rebuilding the small communities that are ravaged most by this epidemic.
